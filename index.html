PREFACE

Setting the scene for women in CS before 1983

1840

The Enchantress

The first computer programmer was female

Ada Lovelace

The Enchantress has thrown her magical spell around the most abstract of Sciences and has grasped it with a force which few masculine intellects could have exerted over it.
- Michael Faraday on Lady Lovelace

Ada Lovelace was born as a disappointment to her father, famed poet Lord Byron.
She was abandoned by her father, who expected a "glorious boy", but was instead presented with a baby girl. Her mother, Anne Isabella Milbanke promoted her interest in math and science from a young age in an attempt to prevent her from developing what she saw as the insanity seen in her father.
Ada's status as Lord Byron's daughter opened the door to higher education, and her ideas and intelligence gained her respect in a field dominated by men.
Her work on Charles Babbage's Analytical Machine would eventually be recognized as the first computer algorithm, making her the first ever computer programmer.

1959

Amazing Grace

The inventor of the first modern coding language was female

Dr. Grace M. Hopper


From the time she was a kid, she showed signs of genius. She would assemble and reassemble clocks, determined to learn the way they work.
During World War II, Hopper voluntarily enlisted to the U.S. Navy and had to request an exemption for being 15lbs under the weight limit. She was sworn into the U.S. Naval Reserve in 1943 and was commissioned as a lieutenant and assigned to the Bureau of Ordinance Computation Project at Harvard University.
In her later career, she released some of the first compiler-based programming languages, MATH-MATIC and FLOW-MATIC. Her belief that that programs should be written in a language that was close to English led to COBOL, the first programming language designed for business use, and the predecessor to modern coding languages.

1967

Women's Work

Computer science and programming was originally seen as "secreterial", i.e. women's work

Women's Work

It's just like planning a dinner, You have to plan ahead and schedule everything so it's ready when you need it. Programming requires patience and the ability to handle detail.
-Dr. Grace Hopper

As late as the 1960s many people perceived computer programming as a natural career choice for savvy young women. Even the trend-spotters at Cosmopolitan Magazine urged their fashionable female readership to consider careers in programming. In an article titled “The Computer Girls,” the magazine described the field as offering better job opportunities for women than many other professional careers.
-Brenda D. Fink

1983

  So what happened?

The Decline


Source: NPR

Up until about the 1980s, computer science was booming in popularity for women.
So... what happened in the early-mid 80s that closed off this field for so many women?
Why has it remained unattractive for women to this day, while other fields are consistently increasing in the number of women they attract?
These are questions of representation that we might never know the definitive answer to, but a few key factors had a direct impact, to this day.

WarGames

The only winning move is not to play..

1984

Revenge Of The Nerds

A shift in representation

Nerd Culture

Their time has come

Revenge Of The Nerds was released and was a hit among the booming nerd subculture of the '80s. It gave a voice to one group, while effectively eclipsing another. The glorification of the "nerd" never included the female nerd, while simultaneously encompassing the representation of a computer scientist

Nerd culture exploded in popularity, and with it came a new, mainstream idea of the computer nerd and the computer scientist.

Certain ideas were, and still are, perpetuated by the representation of the "nerd" in mainstream media.
